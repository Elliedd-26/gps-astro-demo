# Soft Landing

Twelve of us, on cushions, on the floor of someone's Riverdale apartment. 
The host had drawn the curtains halfway — enough light to see each other, 
not so much that anyone felt exposed.

We started with a question that wasn't really a question:

> "What's something you've been carrying that you haven't named yet?"

No one had to answer first. The silence wasn't awkward — it was the kind 
of silence that gives you permission to take a breath. Then someone spoke. 
Then someone else. Then it kept going for almost two hours.

## What this wasn't

This wasn't therapy. None of us pretended to be qualified to fix anything. 
We weren't trained facilitators or social workers (though two of us happen 
to be, off-duty). We didn't take notes. We didn't promise anyone they'd 
feel better by the end.

This also wasn't a vent session. We weren't there to one-up each other's 
pain or compare who had it worse.

What we were trying to do was simpler and harder: **just be in the room 
with each other's mental health, in two languages, without flinching.**

![A close-up of hands holding a warm mug, soft afternoon light](/images/soft-landing/02.svg)
*The host made ginger tea. Someone brought tangyuan. It mattered.*

## What came up

A few themes appeared at different points in the circle, named by different 
people, in both languages:

**The cost of being "the strong one."** Several of us are the eldest 
daughter, the first to immigrate, the one the family calls when things 
break. We laughed about it, then we got quieter.

**Therapy in a second language.** A few people shared what it's like to 
go to therapy in English when your trauma happened in Chinese. The 
specific exhaustion of having to translate before you can even feel.

**The myth of "back home."** What happens when your parents' generation 
imagines China/Hong Kong/Taiwan as the place you should return to "settle 
down," and you know exactly which parts of yourself you'd have to put 
away to do that.

**The grief no one taught us to name.** Coming out grief. Diaspora grief. 
Grief for the version of you your grandmother thought you'd be. Grief 
that doesn't have a word in either language.

![A handwritten note left on the kitchen counter: "thank you for letting me cry here"](/images/soft-landing/03.svg)
*Someone left this on the kitchen counter when they left. It stayed there all evening.*

## What helped

A few small things that the group landed on, not as advice, just as things 
some of us had tried:

- **Bilingual journaling.** Some feelings only come in English. Some only 
  in Chinese. Letting both onto the page, without translating.
- **Finding a therapist who speaks your first language** — even for 
  occasional sessions — if you can. The wait lists in Toronto are long, 
  but they exist.
- **Naming the loop instead of fixing it.** "I'm having the thought that 
  I'm a burden" hits differently than "I am a burden."
- **Touching grass, but make it Chinese herbal.** One person talked about 
  going to the herbalist on Spadina with their mom — not for medicine, 
  for the ritual.

## What's next

We're going to do this again in May. Same format, smaller-scale (12 felt 
right, more than that feels like a workshop).

If you're interested, sign up through our newsletter. We'll do a 
no-questions-asked lottery if more than 12 RSVP.

A few things we're going to keep:
- Phones in the basket at the door
- Two languages, no translation pressure
- The understanding that crying is allowed, leaving is allowed, silence is allowed

If you need mental health support before then — Toronto has bilingual 
options, and we keep a list. DM us anytime.

# 软着陆

我们十二个人，坐在Riverdale一位成员家的客厅地板上，每人一个坐垫。
主人把窗帘半拉着——刚好够我们看见彼此，又不至于让任何人觉得自己被看穿了。

我们以一个不太像问题的问题开场：

> "你最近在背负什么，但还没给它一个名字的？"

没有人需要先回答。那种安静不尴尬——是那种让你可以好好喘口气的安静。
然后有人开口了。然后又有人。然后这件事持续了将近两小时。

## 这不是什么

这不是治疗。我们里没有人假装自己有资格去"修复"什么。
我们不是受过培训的引导员或社工（虽然里面有两位刚好是，下班后的版本）。
我们没有做笔记。我们没有承诺任何人这次结束后会"感觉好一点"。

这也不是抱怨大会。我们不是在比谁更惨。

我们试图做的事更简单也更难：**就是和彼此的心理状态待在同一个房间里，
用两种语言，不退缩。**

![特写：双手捧着温热的马克杯，下午的柔光](/images/soft-landing/02.svg)
*主人煮了姜茶。有人带了汤圆。这些细节是重要的。*

## 我们说了什么

几个主题在不同时刻、由不同的人、用两种语言被命名：

**"做坚强的那个"的代价。** 我们里好几个人都是家里的大女儿、第一个移民出来的人、
家里一出事就给打电话的那个。一开始我们笑着说这件事，后来声音慢慢变小了。

**用第二语言做心理治疗。** 几个人分享了用英语做治疗的疲惫——尤其当你的
trauma是在中文里发生的。那种"必须先翻译，才能感受"的特定的累。

**"回来"这件事的迷思。** 当你父母那一辈想象着中国/香港/台湾是你应该
"回去安定下来"的地方，而你清楚地知道，要做到这件事，你必须把哪些部分的
自己收起来。

**没人教过我们命名的那些哀悼。** 出柜的哀悼。Diaspora的哀悼。
为奶奶想象中那个你的哀悼。那种在哪种语言里都没有词的哀悼。

![厨房台面上一张手写便条："谢谢你让我在这里哭了"](/images/soft-landing/03.svg)
*有人离开的时候把这张纸条留在了厨房。它在台面上待了一整晚。*

## 什么帮到了我们

我们最后聊到一些小事——不是建议，只是一些人试过的方法：

- **双语写日记。** 有些感受只能用英语表达，有些只能用中文。
  让两种语言都流到纸上，不强迫自己翻译。
- **找一个说你母语的咨询师**——哪怕只是偶尔约一次——如果你能找到的话。
  多伦多的wait list很长，但是存在。
- **命名那个loop，而不是修补它。** "我现在有一个'我是负担'的想法"
  跟"我是个负担"完全不一样。
- **接地气，但是用中药的方式。** 有人说她最近会陪妈妈去Spadina的中药铺
  ——不是为了买药，是为了那个仪式感。

## 接下来

我们五月份还会再办一次。同样的形式，控制在小规模（12个人感觉刚好，
再多就像workshop了）。

有兴趣的话通过我们的newsletter报名。如果超过12人，我们会做一个
不问理由的抽签。

有几件事我们会保持：
- 进门把手机放在篮子里
- 两种语言，没有翻译压力
- 哭是允许的，离开是允许的，沉默也是允许的

如果在那之前你需要心理支持——多伦多有一些双语资源，我们有一个list。
随时私信我们。
